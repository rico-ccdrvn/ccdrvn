Mirror & Shoulder Check
1 / 8
Introduction
Why mirror & shoulder checks matter
Mirrors show you what's behind and beside your vehicle — but every car has blind spots mirrors can't reach. A shoulder check is the only way to confirm that space is clear.
On the G2 and G road test, missing a mirror check or shoulder check is an immediate error on any lane change, turn, or merge.
Step Overview
The correct 3-part sequence
Every lane change, merge, and turn requires all three steps — in this exact order. Signal goes on first, then mirrors, then shoulder check before any steering input.
Signal first — it announces your intention to other drivers while you complete your checks. Mirrors and shoulder check happen after the signal is already on.
Step 2 of 3 — Mirrors
Check rearview mirror first
Quick glance to the rearview mirror — get a picture of traffic directly behind you. Is anyone following close? Are you being overtaken?
This glance should take about 1 second — long enough to register, short enough to keep eyes forward most of the time.
Step 2 continued — Mirrors
Then check the side mirror
Shift your eyes to the left side mirror (moving left) or right side mirror (moving right). You'll see traffic approaching from behind in that lane.
The side mirror still can't see vehicles right beside you. That's the blind spot — only the shoulder check clears it.
Step 1 of 3 — Signal
Signal first — minimum 3 seconds
Signal on first — before you check mirrors or move. This gives other road users notice of your intention while you complete the remaining checks.
Signal for a minimum of 3 seconds before the maneuver begins. Examiners watch for this — a late or missing signal is an immediate error.
Step 3 of 3 — Shoulder Check
Chin to shoulder — clear the blind spot
Turn your head — chin toward the shoulder on the side you're moving to. You're looking for one thing: a vehicle already in the lane you're merging or turning into, coming from two lanes over.
This is a targeted glance, not a full look-around. Head must visibly turn — examiners watch for head movement, not just eye movement. Clear blind spot, then steer.
What Goes Wrong
Common errors to avoid
  • ✕
    Mirrors before signal — checking mirrors first, then signalling. Signal goes on first, then mirrors.
  • ✕
    Eye glance only — moving eyes without turning the head. Examiner can't see it; blind spot isn't cleared.
  • ✕
    Checking the wrong shoulder — turning right but checking left. Common on stress-filled test day.
  • ✕
    Steering while checking — vehicle starts to drift during the shoulder check. Check first, then steer.
  • ✕
    Skipping the check at low speed — required at all speeds, including parking lot maneuvers.
